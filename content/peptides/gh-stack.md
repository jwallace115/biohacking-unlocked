# The GH Stack: CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin

*Last updated: April 2026*

The GH Stack caught on because the idea is simple, but the biology behind it is actually pretty smart.

Your pituitary has more than one way to release growth hormone.

CJC-1295 without DAC presses one button.

Ipamorelin presses another.

Press both at the same time, and you can create a stronger GH pulse than either one produces alone.

That is the core idea behind the CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin stack. And unlike some peptide combinations that feel like two popular names got stapled together, this one has a real pharmacology story behind it.

The community did not invent the idea that GHRH and GHRP pathways work together. Researchers studying growth hormone secretion have known for a long time that those two pathways can be synergistic.

That is the positive case.

The honest caveat is just as important: there are no published human clinical outcomes trials showing that this specific stack improves body composition, recovery, sleep, or anti-aging markers in healthy adults.

So the clean framing is this:

The GH pulse logic is real.
The clinical outcome claims are not proven.

Both things can be true.

Where I am stating a fact, I am citing it. Where I am sharing my read on the research, I am saying that out loud.

## What the GH Stack actually is

The GH Stack usually refers to:

- CJC-1295 without DAC
- Ipamorelin

That "without DAC" part matters.

CJC-1295 without DAC is also commonly called Mod GRF 1-29 or Modified GRF 1-29. It is a short-acting GHRH analog with a roughly 30-minute half-life. It produces a brief GH signal and clears quickly.

That is the version the standard community GH Stack is built around.

CJC-1295 with DAC is a different pharmacological situation. The DAC version binds albumin and stays active for days, creating a more sustained GH elevation. That is not what most people mean when they talk about the classic CJC-1295 + Ipamorelin stack.

Ipamorelin is a selective growth hormone secretagogue. It works through the ghrelin receptor, also called GHS-R1a. Compared with older GHRPs like GHRP-2, GHRP-6, and hexarelin, Ipamorelin is known for stimulating GH with less cortisol and prolactin spillover.

So the stack is not "two peptides doing the exact same thing."

It is two different receptor pathways aimed at the same output: a GH pulse.

The stack is commonly discussed for:

- Body recomposition
- Sleep quality
- Recovery
- Anti-aging
- General GH optimization

## Why two switches instead of one

This is the section that makes the GH Stack worth its own page.

Growth hormone release can be stimulated through more than one pathway.

The two relevant ones here are:

- The GHRH receptor pathway
- The ghrelin / GHS-R1a receptor pathway

CJC-1295 without DAC hits the first pathway.

Ipamorelin hits the second.

**The GHRH receptor pathway**

GHRH stands for growth hormone-releasing hormone.

This is the signal your hypothalamus naturally uses to tell the pituitary to release GH. CJC-1295 without DAC mimics that signal with a modified, short-acting peptide.

Think of it as the body's classic GH-release instruction.

**The ghrelin receptor pathway**

Ghrelin is best known as a hunger hormone, but it also helps stimulate GH release.

Ipamorelin mimics part of that ghrelin signal at the GHS-R1a receptor. The reason Ipamorelin became popular is that it is more selective than older GHRPs. It can stimulate GH without the same level of cortisol, prolactin, and appetite effects that made older compounds messier.

Think of this as a second GH-release instruction coming from a different lane.

**The synergy**

When both pathways are activated together, the GH pulse can be larger than either pathway produces alone.

That is the stack logic.

One pathway tells the pituitary, "release GH."
The other pathway tells the pituitary, "yes, release more GH."

That is oversimplified, but it captures the practical idea.

This is not the same kind of stack as BPC-157 + TB-500. The Wolverine Stack is two different repair angles. The GH Stack is two switches on the same system.

That is why it feels tighter mechanistically.

**Why pulsatility matters**

The body does not release GH in a flat line all day.

It releases GH in pulses, especially during deep sleep.

That is why the community usually prefers CJC-1295 without DAC for this stack. The short-acting version fits the pulse concept better. It comes in, helps trigger the signal, and then clears.

The DAC version is more complicated because it keeps GH elevated much longer. That may be pharmacologically interesting, but it is not the classic GH Stack concept.

Plain English: CJC-1295 without DAC presses the GHRH button. Ipamorelin presses the ghrelin button. Together, they aim for a stronger GH pulse while still respecting the body's pulsing rhythm.

That is the appeal.

## What the individual research shows

The GH Stack is built from two separate research stories.

**CJC-1295**

CJC-1295 has human pharmacology data showing that it can raise GH and IGF-1.

**Teichman et al. (2006)** *JCEM* (PMID 16352683) studied CJC-1295 in healthy adults and found dose-dependent increases in GH and IGF-1. That is the pharmacology story: CJC-1295 can move the GH axis in humans.

**Ionescu and Frohman (2006)** *JCEM* (PMID 17018654) looked at the GH secretion pattern with CJC-1295. The key finding: CJC-1295 elevated trough and mean GH secretion while preserving pulsatility — though the elevated baseline changes the overall GH environment even between pulses.

The safety story matters too.

A Phase II clinical trial of CJC-1295 in lipodystrophy was halted after a participant death. The relationship to the drug was investigated.

FDA has also identified serious adverse events with CJC-1295, including increased heart rate and systemic vasodilatory reaction.

That belongs in the conversation.

**Ipamorelin**

Ipamorelin's strongest evidence is its GH-release selectivity.

**Raun et al. (1998)** *Eur J Endocrinol* (PMID 9849822) characterized Ipamorelin as a potent and selective GH secretagogue, with minimal cortisol and prolactin elevation compared with older GHRPs.

That is why Ipamorelin became the preferred GHRP in the biohacking community.

The main large human trial wrinkle is **Beck et al. (2014)**, a Phase 2 trial in postoperative ileus. That trial did not meet its primary endpoint and development for that indication was discontinued. That negative trial does not erase the GH-release mechanism. It does show, once again, that pharmacology does not automatically translate into clinical success for a chosen indication.

**The combination**

There are no published human clinical outcomes trials testing CJC-1295 without DAC plus Ipamorelin for body composition, recovery, sleep, or anti-aging in healthy adults.

The synergy concept is real at the pharmacodynamic level. GHRH plus GHRP pathways can amplify GH release. But the specific community stack is not clinically proven as an outcomes protocol.

That distinction is everything.

## What the community protocol looks like

Community-reported protocols — not endorsements.

The standard GH Stack protocol usually looks like this:

- CJC-1295 without DAC: 100 mcg per dose
- Ipamorelin: 100 mcg per dose
- Route: subcutaneous injection
- Frequency: 1–3 times daily
- Timing: pre-bed dosing is the most common
- Cycle pattern: often 8–16 weeks on, followed by time off

The two peptides are often combined in the same injection.

The pre-bed timing is not random. The body's largest natural GH pulse typically happens during the first part of deep sleep. The community logic is to time the stack before bed and try to amplify the body's natural overnight pulse instead of creating a random daytime signal. That is physiologically coherent — not proven to produce better long-term outcomes than other timing windows, but the reasoning makes sense.

Some people also discuss morning fasted dosing or post-workout timing. But the pre-bed version is the most common because it lines up with natural GH rhythm.

Community protocols only. Not validated medical dosing.

A few hard avoidance categories worth stating plainly: active or recent cancer, uncontrolled diabetes, untreated or significant sleep apnea, pregnancy or breastfeeding, and anyone under 18 or not past skeletal maturity. GH-axis manipulation raises specific concerns in all of those situations.

## The safety picture

This stack deserves a dedicated safety section because of the CJC-1295 side.

The GH Stack is not in the same risk category as a topical cosmetic peptide. It is also not the same kind of risk discussion as the Wolverine Stack. This is endocrine signaling.

**CJC-1295-specific concerns**

A Phase II trial of CJC-1295 in lipodystrophy was halted after a participant death. The relationship to the drug was investigated.

FDA has also flagged serious adverse events for CJC-1295, including increased heart rate and systemic vasodilatory reaction.

That does not mean every person using CJC-1295 will have a serious event. It means the safety profile deserves a serious read.

**GH-axis class concerns**

Both compounds are aimed at increasing GH release. That brings the broader GH/IGF-1 questions into the conversation.

IGF-1 and cancer: IGF-1 elevation is epidemiologically associated with several cancer types. That does not prove this stack causes cancer. But it is a reason the question cannot be dismissed, especially with long-term use.

Glucose metabolism: Growth hormone signaling can affect insulin sensitivity. That matters for anyone with diabetes risk, metabolic syndrome, or poor glucose control.

Sleep apnea: GH-axis manipulation and fluid shifts can be relevant for people with untreated sleep apnea.

Outcome claims: Better body composition, improved recovery, better sleep, and anti-aging effects are the claims people care about. Those outcomes have not been proven in controlled trials for this combination.

**Ipamorelin-specific context**

Ipamorelin is generally viewed as cleaner than older GHRPs because of its minimal cortisol and prolactin elevation. The most commonly reported immediate effects are flushing, head rush, or transient sensations after injection.

My read: Ipamorelin is the cleaner half of the stack. CJC-1295 is the part that makes the safety discussion heavier. That does not kill the stack concept. It just means the stack deserves more respect than casual "anti-aging peptide" marketing gives it.

## The regulatory situation (April 2026)

CJC-1295 and Ipamorelin are both not FDA approved.

Both have been part of FDA's compounding-review conversation, including PCAC discussions around the October and December 2024 meetings.

The post-April 2026 Category 2 status for both should be verified against the current FDA docket. The April 2026 removal list primarily covered different compounds.

The clean practical framing: CJC-1295 is not FDA approved. Ipamorelin is not FDA approved. Both are commonly sold as research chemicals. Their regulatory status has been under FDA scrutiny.

WADA is much clearer.

CJC-1295 is explicitly prohibited under S2.2.4 as a GHRH analog — named alongside CJC-1293, sermorelin, and tesamorelin.

Ipamorelin is also explicitly prohibited under S2.2.4 as a growth hormone secretagogue.

For tested athletes, the GH Stack is not a gray area.

In the United States, both compounds are sold through the research-chemical market. "Research use only" is legal framing, not a safety guarantee.

## The purity problem

The purity problem is sharper with stacks. One compound means one quality-control chain. Two compounds means two.

With the GH Stack, the CJC-1295 naming issue makes this especially important.

A vial labeled "CJC-1295" could be with DAC, without DAC, mislabeled, underdosed, or backed by a generic non-matching COA. That matters because the standard GH Stack uses without DAC. If someone thinks they are using without DAC but actually has the with-DAC version, they are getting a much longer-acting GH signal than intended. That is not a minor labeling mistake — it changes the pharmacology of the protocol.

Ipamorelin has its own verification needs too. Since it is usually paired with CJC-1295, a purity or identity problem in either vial compromises the whole stack.

For both peptides, the COA checklist should include:

- Does the COA match the exact batch?
- Does mass spectrometry confirm identity?
- Is purity measured by HPLC?
- Is the testing from a real third-party lab?
- Are sterility and endotoxin addressed?
- Does the CJC-1295 COA specify with DAC or without DAC?
- Does the vendor avoid claims that contradict research-use-only labeling?

A COA is not decoration. It is the receipt. And with the GH Stack, the CJC receipt needs to say the right version.

## Bottom line

My honest read: the GH Stack is one of the most logical community protocols in the peptide world.

The two-switch idea is real pharmacology. CJC-1295 without DAC activates the GHRH receptor. Ipamorelin activates the ghrelin receptor. Both converge on GH release. Together, they can produce a stronger GH pulse than either pathway alone.

That is why the stack makes sense.

The without-DAC choice is also important. It keeps the concept closer to the body's natural pulsatile GH rhythm instead of using the long-acting DAC version that changes the signal for days.

That is the positive case, and it is a strong one.

But the outcome gap is still real. Moving GH is pharmacodynamics. Proving better body composition, recovery, sleep, or anti-aging effects in healthy adults is clinical outcomes research. That has not been done for this specific stack.

The safety picture also deserves respect. The CJC-1295 Phase II trial event and FDA-flagged cardiovascular reactions put this stack in a more serious category than casual peptide marketing suggests.

So the clean read is this:

The GH Stack is mechanistically coherent, pharmacologically interesting, and one of the better-reasoned community stacks. It is also unproven as a healthy-adult outcomes protocol, prohibited for tested athletes, and dependent on very careful product verification.

Not hype. Not dismissal.

A smart idea that still needs the kind of human outcome data it has not gotten yet.
