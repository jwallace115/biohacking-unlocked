The Wolverine Stack: BPC-157 + TB-500

Last updated: April 2026

The Wolverine Stack might be the most famous peptide combination in the biohacking world.

BPC-157 plus TB-500.

If someone is researching peptides for soft-tissue repair, tendon problems, ligament issues, muscle injuries, or general recovery, this is the stack they usually run into first.

The name tells you a lot.

"Wolverine" is a reference to the Marvel character known for rapid healing. That is the community aspiration. Faster recovery. Better tissue repair. A feeling that the body has more repair support when something is injured or irritated.

And honestly, I understand why this stack caught on.

The logic is not random.

BPC-157 and TB-500 are both discussed in tissue-repair contexts, but they are usually discussed as working from different angles. BPC-157 is often framed around angiogenesis, local repair signaling, and injury-site environment. TB-500 is usually framed around cell migration, actin regulation, and broader tissue remodeling.

That is a coherent pairing on paper.

But here is the important line:

There are no published controlled human trials testing BPC-157 and TB-500 together.

The stack is a community-built hypothesis based on two separate research literatures.

That does not make it nonsense. It does mean the evidence has to be read correctly.

Where I am stating a fact, I am citing it. Where I am sharing my read on the research, I am saying that out loud.

What the Wolverine Stack actually is

The Wolverine Stack is the community name for combining:

* BPC-157
* TB-500

BPC-157 stands for Body Protection Compound-157. It is a 15-amino-acid synthetic peptide derived from a protective protein sequence found in human gastric juice. In peptide communities, it is mostly discussed for tendon, ligament, gut, muscle, and general tissue-repair contexts.

TB-500 is a synthetic peptide marketed as related to thymosin beta-4, a naturally occurring 43-amino-acid peptide involved in actin regulation, cell migration, and tissue repair. One important caveat: most of the deeper research literature is on full thymosin beta-4, not always the exact short TB-500 fragment sold by research vendors.

That distinction matters, and we will come back to it.

The mechanistic logic — why these two together

This is the part that makes the Wolverine Stack worth understanding.

BPC-157 and TB-500 are not usually described as doing the exact same thing. They are described as helping different parts of the repair process.

That is why the stack caught on.

BPC-157's proposed role

BPC-157 is usually discussed around the local injury environment.

In animal models, BPC-157 has been studied for:

* Angiogenesis, meaning new blood vessel formation
* Tendon and ligament healing
* Gut repair
* Muscle injury recovery
* Nitric oxide modulation
* Local tissue repair signaling

The easiest way to think about BPC-157 is infrastructure.

Injury sites need blood supply. They need signaling. They need the local environment to support repair.

BPC-157 is often discussed as a peptide that may help create a better repair environment, at least in animal models.

That is why the tendon and ligament world took notice. Tendons and ligaments are notoriously slow to heal because blood supply is not great. Anything that appears to affect vascular and repair signaling is going to get attention.

TB-500's proposed role

TB-500 is usually discussed around cell movement and tissue remodeling.

The thymosin beta-4 research world focuses heavily on actin regulation. Actin is part of the cellular skeleton that helps cells move, change shape, and respond to injury.

That matters because repair is not just about signals existing in a tissue. Repair also requires the right cells to move into the right place and do the work.

TB-500 is discussed for:

* Actin regulation
* Cell migration
* Tissue remodeling
* Wound-healing biology
* Anti-inflammatory signaling
* Broader repair coordination

The easiest way to think about TB-500 is cellular response.

If BPC-157 is often framed as improving the repair environment, TB-500 is often framed as helping repair-related cells move and respond inside that environment.

Why the pairing makes sense

This is the stack logic:

BPC-157 may help improve the infrastructure of repair.
TB-500 may help support the cellular response.

One is the roadwork.
The other is the crew moving through the site.

That is not a perfect scientific analogy, but it is a useful plain-English way to understand why people combine them.

My honest read: the Wolverine Stack is one of the more mechanistically coherent community protocols in the peptide world. The idea has a logic to it. Two different angles on tissue repair, potentially complementary.

The missing piece is controlled research testing the combination.

The mechanism makes sense as a hypothesis. It is not yet proven as a human protocol.

What the individual research shows

This section needs to stay clean.

There is research on BPC-157 by itself.
There is research on thymosin beta-4 and TB-500-related biology by itself.
There is community reporting on the combination.

Those are three different things.

BPC-157

BPC-157 has decades of animal research behind it, especially in rodent models involving gut injury, tendon injury, ligament repair, muscle damage, blood vessel formation, and other tissue-repair contexts.

That is the strong side of the BPC-157 story.

The human evidence is much thinner.

A 2025 systematic review by Vasireddi et al. screened hundreds of articles and found that the included evidence was overwhelmingly preclinical: 35 preclinical studies and one retrospective clinical study involving 12 patients. That is a major calibration point.

BPC-157 is one of the more interesting animal-research peptides on this site. But the human data is still early.

TB-500 / thymosin beta-4

TB-500 has a different evidence problem.

The deeper research literature is mostly on full thymosin beta-4, not always the commercial TB-500 fragment people buy from research vendors.

That matters.

Full thymosin beta-4 is a naturally occurring 43-amino-acid peptide. TB-500 is commonly marketed as a synthetic fragment related to that molecule. The community often talks about them as if they are interchangeable, but they are not automatically the same thing.

The animal wound-healing literature on thymosin beta-4 is genuinely interesting. Malinda et al. (1999) reported that thymosin beta-4 accelerated wound healing in a rat model, including increased re-epithelialization reported at day 4 and day 7.

There is also human wound-healing research on thymosin beta-4. Treadwell et al. (2012) studied thymosin beta-4 in Phase 2 wound-healing trials. That is useful human context, but the hedge matters: this was full thymosin beta-4, not necessarily the same TB-500 fragment sold online.

So the TB-500 story has two layers:

The thymosin beta-4 biology is real and worth paying attention to.
The commercial TB-500 fragment has less direct human evidence.

The combination

Here is the cleanest possible statement:

There are no published controlled human trials testing BPC-157 and TB-500 together.

There are also no strong published animal trials specifically validating the combination as a stack.

The Wolverine Stack is community-built.

It is built on a plausible interpretation of two separate research literatures, but the stack itself has not been clinically tested.

That distinction matters.

My read: the individual research gives the stack a reason to exist as a hypothesis. It does not prove the stack works as a protocol.

What the community protocol looks like

Community-reported protocols — not endorsements.

The most commonly discussed Wolverine Stack pattern looks something like this:

* BPC-157: 250–500 mcg per day
* TB-500: 2–5 mg per week
* Route: usually subcutaneous injection
* TB-500 timing: often split into two weekly doses
* Duration: often 4–8 weeks as a "loading" phase
* Use case: active injury, tendon/ligament problems, muscle strains, post-surgical recovery

Some users report injecting BPC-157 near the injury site when possible. TB-500 is usually treated more systemically in community discussions.

Some people run the stack only during injury or recovery periods. Others use it more broadly for ongoing recovery support.

No validated medical dosing standard exists for either compound, and definitely not for the combination.

That is worth saying plainly.

The community built the protocol around practical use: one daily, one weekly, both aimed at repair. That structure is one reason the stack became easy to talk about and easy to remember.

But easy to remember is not the same as clinically validated.

My read: the community protocol is coherent enough to understand why it spread. But the research has not caught up with the protocol.

What the stack does not have

This section is important because the name "Wolverine Stack" can make the combination sound more established than it is.

Here is what it does not have.

No human combination trial data

Zero published controlled human trials testing BPC-157 plus TB-500 together.

That is the big one.

Every research claim has to come from one of three places:

* BPC-157 alone
* Thymosin beta-4 / TB-500-related research alone
* Community reports

That is not the same as combination evidence.

Very thin BPC-157 human data

BPC-157 has a large animal-research footprint, but human evidence is still limited. The Vasireddi 2025 systematic review is a useful reality check because it shows how much of the evidence is still preclinical.

That does not make BPC-157 boring. It makes the translation question real.

TB-500 identity confusion

TB-500 is often treated as if it is the same thing as thymosin beta-4.

That is not clean.

Full thymosin beta-4 and commercial TB-500 fragments are not automatically interchangeable. The strongest human data belongs more to the thymosin beta-4 side of the literature than to the exact research-market TB-500 products people buy online.

That gap matters.

No proof that stacking fixes the evidence gaps

This is a big one.

If BPC-157 alone has limited human evidence, and TB-500 as sold commercially has identity and evidence questions, combining them does not automatically solve those problems.

Stacking can create a better hypothesis.

It does not create clinical proof.

Both compounds are prohibited for tested athletes

BPC-157 and TB-500 are both WADA prohibited, but under different categories.

BPC-157 has been prohibited under S0 as a non-approved substance.

TB-500 falls under S2, where thymosin-beta-4 and its derivatives are prohibited.

For tested athletes, the Wolverine Stack is not a gray area.

The regulatory situation (April 2026)

Both peptides in the Wolverine Stack are in active regulatory territory.

BPC-157

BPC-157 is not FDA approved for any indication.

It was removed from FDA Category 2 effective April 22, 2026 after nominator withdrawal. That does not mean FDA approved it. Category 2 removal is not a safety finding, efficacy finding, or green light for broad human use.

BPC-157 is scheduled for FDA Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee consultation on July 23, 2026.

BPC-157 is also WADA prohibited under S0.

TB-500

TB-500 is not FDA approved for any indication.

It was also removed from FDA Category 2 effective April 22, 2026 after nominator withdrawal. Like BPC-157, that is not FDA approval.

TB-500 is also scheduled for PCAC consultation on July 23, 2026.

WADA prohibits thymosin-beta-4 and its derivatives under S2, which includes the TB-500 concern.

That shared July 23, 2026 date is worth watching.

For the Wolverine Stack, both compounds are moving through the same FDA advisory window at the same time. Whatever happens there will shape the next version of the regulatory story.

My read: this is one of the most interesting regulatory watch points on the site. The stack is community-built, but both ingredients are actively being reviewed in the U.S. compounding conversation.

The purity problem — doubled

The purity problem is bigger with stacks.

With one peptide, you have one vial, one COA, one batch number, one identity question, one sterility question, and one vendor-quality question.

With the Wolverine Stack, you have two.

That means two opportunities for:

* Mislabeling
* Underdosing
* Contamination
* Fake COAs
* Old COAs
* Batch mismatches
* Identity problems
* Vendor inconsistency

And TB-500 adds an extra issue: identity.

A vial labeled "TB-500" may not always map cleanly onto the thymosin beta-4 research literature. The fragment question matters. The sequence matters. The COA needs to identify what is actually in the vial.

Running TB-500 with BPC-157 does not fix that problem.

It stacks on top of it.

For both peptides, the COA checklist should include:

* Does the COA match the exact batch?
* Does mass spectrometry confirm identity?
* Is purity measured by HPLC?
* Is the testing from a real third-party lab?
* Are sterility and endotoxin addressed for injectable products?
* Is the vendor using clean research-use-only language?
* Are there health claims that contradict the RUO posture?
* Does the TB-500 COA identify the actual fragment or sequence?

A COA is not decoration. It is the receipt.

And with a stack, you need two receipts.

Bottom line

My honest read: the Wolverine Stack is one of the more compelling community-built peptide ideas.

The name is dramatic, but the underlying logic is not silly.

BPC-157 and TB-500 are both discussed in tissue-repair contexts, and they are usually discussed from different mechanistic angles. BPC-157 is the local repair-environment peptide. TB-500 is the cell-migration and tissue-remodeling peptide.

That pairing makes sense as a hypothesis.

And I get why people are excited about it.

When you are dealing with tendon, ligament, muscle, or post-surgical recovery issues, the idea of supporting tissue repair from two angles is naturally appealing. This is one of those stacks where the community logic is easy to understand.

But the evidence line has to stay clean.

The individual compounds have interesting research behind them, especially in animal models and thymosin-beta-4 wound-healing literature. The combination itself has not been tested in controlled human trials.

That means the Wolverine Stack is a hypothesis, not a proven protocol.

That is not a negative take. It is the honest take.

The stack is coherent. The biology is interesting. The community enthusiasm makes sense. The regulatory story is active. The quality-control burden is doubled. And the human combination evidence is not there yet.

If BPC-157 and TB-500 are two of the more interesting tissue-repair peptides on their own, the stack is an interesting next question.

But combining two early-stage peptides does not magically turn them into settled medicine.

It turns them into a bigger hypothesis.

And this is one of the peptide hypotheses I would most like to see properly studied.
