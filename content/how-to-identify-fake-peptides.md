# How to Identify Fake Peptides and Bad Vendors

*Last updated: April 2026*

The gray-market peptide world has vendors who take quality seriously and vendors who are running a convincing imitation of a vendor who takes quality seriously.

The difference is not always obvious from the outside.

This page documents the patterns that separate real vendors from bad ones — drawn from direct research into vendor practices, community testing reports, and documented cases of fraud and regulatory enforcement in the peptide market.

None of this is abstract. Every pattern here has a real example behind it.

## The first rule

If a vendor cannot show you a current, batch-specific COA from a named third-party laboratory, the conversation is over.

Everything else on this page assumes a vendor has at least cleared that bar. Most of the patterns below are about what to look for after you have the COA in hand.

For a full explanation of how to evaluate a COA, read [How to Verify a Peptide COA](/peptide-coa-explained).

## Red flag patterns

### 1. Verification key removed from the COA

Janoshik Analytical is the gold-standard third-party testing lab in the peptide community. Their COAs include a verification key — a code that links directly to Janoshik's portal so anyone can confirm the COA was actually issued by Janoshik for that batch.

Some fake COAs have had this verification key physically removed, smudged, or cropped out.

If you see a COA that claims to be from Janoshik but has no verification key, it cannot be confirmed as real.

**What to do:** Go to janoshik.com and try to verify the COA directly. If the key is missing, treat the COA as unverified.

### 2. Certificate ID reused across vendors or products

A real COA is batch-specific. The certificate ID is unique to one vendor, one product, one batch.

In documented cases, vendors have used the same certificate ID on COAs for different products or sold COAs that match another vendor's certificate ID for a completely different batch.

**What to do:** Search the certificate ID number online. If you find the same ID on a different vendor's site or attached to a different product, the COA has been reused. That is a forgery signal.

### 3. "In-house" lab with no independent presence

Some vendors claim to test their own products in-house or name a testing laboratory that has no website, no phone number, no verifiable address, and no online presence.

That is not third-party testing. That is the vendor issuing their own certificate about their own product.

**What to do:** Search the lab name. If the lab does not have an independent online presence and no verification portal, the COA cannot be independently confirmed.

### 4. Fake third-party verification infrastructure

Some bad actors have gone further — building fake verification portals that mimic the look of legitimate lab sites. These sites appear to confirm COAs but are controlled by the vendor or an affiliate.

The documented case in our research: ggpeps.net created fake verification infrastructure that mimicked Janoshik's portal format.

**What to do:** When verifying a COA, use the actual lab's known URL directly — not a link on the vendor's site. If the lab is Janoshik, go to janoshik.com directly. If the lab is Freedom Diagnostics, go to freedomdiagnosticstesting.com directly. Do not follow vendor-provided links to verification portals.

### 5. Health claims contradicting research-use disclaimers

Vendors in the peptide market are legally required to sell these compounds under "research use only" labeling. Many also include disclaimers stating products are "not for human consumption."

Some vendors maintain those disclaimers while simultaneously publishing blog posts, social media content, or product copy that describes specific human health benefits — "accelerates tissue repair," "burns fat," "boosts growth hormone."

That contradiction is a compliance red flag.

The strict RUO disclaimer is a legal shield. If the same vendor's marketing reads like a pharmaceutical advertisement, FDA likely treats them as making unapproved drug claims regardless of the disclaimer.

**What to do:** Read the vendor's blog, social media, and product descriptions alongside their legal disclaimers. If the marketing reads like a drug claim while the footer says "not for human use," that is an intentional contradiction.

### 6. Prices significantly below market

Peptide synthesis has real costs. High-quality testing has real costs. A vendor who prices significantly below competitors is either accepting lower margins or cutting corners somewhere.

Documented patterns include vendors selling peptides at prices 30–50% below market that were later found to be underdosed, mislabeled, or never shipped.

**What to do:** Know the approximate market price range for standard peptides. BPC-157 5mg, TB-500 5mg, CJC-1295, Ipamorelin — these have fairly consistent price floors across quality vendors. Prices that look dramatically better than everyone else should raise a question, not trigger a click.

### 7. Tablets, capsules, or consumer-format products

Real research peptides are sold as lyophilized powder in vials, or as reconstituted solutions in vials. They require careful storage, reconstitution, and handling.

Peptides sold in tablets, capsules, or consumer supplement-style packaging are designed for convenient human consumption — which directly contradicts the "research use only" framing.

Documented cases in the market include vendors facing federal criminal charges after selling peptides in tablet form marketed as dietary supplements.

**What to do:** If a vendor's peptide catalog includes tablet or capsule formats, treat their "research use only" framing as a legal fiction.

### 8. Affiliate marketing launched before testing is complete

There is a documented pattern in the peptide community of vendors launching affiliate programs, recruiting content creators, and building public sales channels before completing their testing infrastructure — including endotoxin and sterility testing for injectable products.

One documented community report describes a vendor owner stating they planned to complete endotoxin and heavy metal testing "after some sales."

**What to do:** Ask vendors directly about their testing. A vendor who has not completed endotoxin and sterility testing for injectable products is treating buyers as the testing population.

### 9. Trustpilot profile removed

Trustpilot does not typically remove vendor profiles for trivial reasons. Common reasons include vendor-incentivized review schemes, manipulation of review scores, or patterns suggesting fake reviews.

A removed Trustpilot profile is not automatic proof of fraud. It is a flag worth investigating before purchasing.

**What to do:** Before purchasing from a vendor, search their Trustpilot profile directly. If it has been removed or shows a "profile removed" message, investigate why before proceeding.

### 10. Crypto-only or unusual payment methods

Vendors who have been refused by major credit card processors often move to crypto-only or use workarounds like digital gift certificates, Zelle, or Cash App.

Payment processors refuse service for compliance reasons. A vendor who cannot accept credit cards is often one who could not satisfy processor requirements around legal product status, chargebacks, or regulatory risk.

Additionally: crypto payments have no chargeback protection. If a vendor does not ship, you have no recourse.

**What to do:** If a vendor only accepts crypto, ask why. Their answer will tell you something.

### 11. No physical address

A vendor with no verifiable physical address has no accountability mechanism. There is no legal entity to contact, no state registration to verify, no physical location tied to their operation.

The most credible vendors in the market have real addresses — not just a PO box or a virtual office, but a genuine business registration and physical location.

**What to do:** Search the vendor's address. Check whether it resolves to a registered business. A virtual office is a yellow flag. No address at all is a red one.

### 12. COAs older than 12 months on injectable products

Peptides degrade over time. A COA from 18 months ago does not tell you what is in the vial you are receiving today. Current, batch-specific testing matters.

Vendors who recycle old COAs across current inventory may be saving on testing costs. They may also have no current data on what they are selling.

**What to do:** Check the date on every COA. For injectable peptide products, anything older than 12 months should prompt a question to the vendor. Older than 18–24 months without explanation is a red flag.

## The enforcement context

The peptide market has seen real federal enforcement actions. These are not theoretical risks.

Documented cases from 2022–2026 include:

- **All American Peptide** — federal forfeiture of over $3 million
- **Tailor Made Compounding** — federal criminal conviction
- **Paradigm Peptides** — federal criminal plea (December 2025)
- **Amino Asylum** — alleged FDA raid (June 2025), domain fragmentation
- **Peptide Sciences** — voluntary shutdown (March 2026), with subsequent impersonator domains appearing within weeks

The enforcement pattern has included seizures, criminal charges, and civil forfeiture. It has targeted vendors selling compounds with explicit health claims, vendors selling to athletic populations, and vendors operating under "research use only" framing while directing human use.

Understanding the enforcement context helps explain why vendor quality and compliance posture matter beyond just personal risk.

## The short version

A vendor worth using can answer yes to all of these:

- Is there a current, batch-specific COA from a named third-party lab?
- Does the COA include identity confirmation by mass spectrometry?
- Can the COA be independently verified through the lab's portal?
- Does the batch number on the COA match the vial?
- Is the COA dated within the last 12 months?
- Does the vendor have a real address and customer service contact?
- Is the vendor's marketing consistent with their legal disclaimers?
- Has the vendor been in operation for at least 2 years without major red flags?
