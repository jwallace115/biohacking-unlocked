# Biohacking Unlocked — Peptide Voice Template v1

**Status:** Locked April 2026
**Use case:** Apply to all peptide content pages, comparison pages, problem-focused guides, vendor reviews, and cautionary content
**Document purpose:** Single source of truth for editorial voice. Future content briefs reference this template by name rather than restating it.

---

## Core identity

Biohacking Unlocked is a research-informed interpretation site.

We are not pretending to be doctors, academics, or a medical journal. We are curious people doing serious research, showing our work, and giving our honest read in plain English.

The goal is not to encourage or discourage peptide use. The goal is to help adults understand what is known, what is still early, what the community is doing, what regulators are saying, and what practical questions matter before anyone makes their own decision.

---

## The writer

The writer is:

- Curious
- Plain-spoken
- Research-obsessed
- Positive about interesting biology
- Honest about uncertainty
- Respectful of the reader's intelligence
- Not afraid to have an opinion
- Not trying to sound like a doctor
- Not trying to sound like a supplement company

**Think:**

A regular working-class person who got deeply interested in biohacking, started researching everything they could find, and now shares the research in plain English with other curious adults.

**Not:**

A clinician, professor, lawyer, influencer, guru, or supplement salesman.

---

## Editorial position

The site separates facts from interpretation.

A fact is stated cleanly:

> BPC-157 has fewer than 60 published human participants across available human studies.

An interpretation is owned openly:

> My honest read: the human data is early, but the animal research and mechanism make this one worth watching.

The writer is allowed to have a point of view. The writer is allowed to be positive, interested, impressed, skeptical, excited, or unconvinced — as long as they do not invent facts or present opinion as proof.

### Site-wide editorial principle

Use this idea often, especially in early pages:

> Where I am stating a fact, I am citing it. Where I am sharing my read on the research, I am saying that out loud.

This is the trust contract with the reader.

## Tone calibration

### Correct tone

Curious, positive, plain-English, and honest.

Examples:

- "BPC-157 is one of the more interesting peptides I have come across."
- "The biology is unusual. The mechanism makes sense to me. The animal data is hard to ignore."
- "The human research is early, but I do not think early automatically means worthless — early means early."
- "There is a real difference between 'this peptide is overhyped marketing nonsense' and 'this peptide has real biology and is in the early innings of human research.'"
- "That is the kind of biology that makes me lean in rather than tune out."

### Wrong tone: too academic

Avoid writing like this:

> BPC-157 demonstrates significant efficacy in promoting tissue regeneration through angiogenic mechanisms, with substantial preclinical evidence supporting its therapeutic potential.

**Why it is wrong:** too clinical, too detached, sounds like a journal abstract.

### Wrong tone: too cautious

Avoid writing like this:

> The human evidence is extremely limited, and therefore no meaningful conclusions can be drawn regarding BPC-157's therapeutic value.

**Why it is wrong:** technically safe but not the Biohacking Unlocked voice. It sounds like a legal memo and dismisses interesting early research too quickly.

### Wrong tone: too salesy

Avoid writing like this:

> BPC-157 is a powerful healing peptide trusted by athletes and biohackers worldwide.

**Why it is wrong:** sounds like vendor copy and implies human benefit.

## The "paramedic frame"

This is the core calibration rule.

A paramedic is not a doctor, but they understand enough medicine to have informed opinions, recognize patterns, explain things clearly, and know when something is interesting.

**That is the voice.**

The writer is allowed to say:

- "This mechanism makes sense to me."
- "This is the part I find most compelling."
- "I can see why the community took notice."
- "This is one I would keep watching."

But the writer does not say:

- "This treats injuries."
- "This is proven safe."
- "You should use this."
- "This protocol works."

---

## Curiosity-first rule

Lead with what makes the peptide interesting, not what makes it risky.

The opening hook should usually answer:

- Why are people talking about this peptide?
- What biology makes it interesting?
- Why did this send us down a research rabbit hole?
- What makes it different from generic supplement hype?

**Example:**

> BPC-157 is one of the more interesting peptides I have come across. It has 30+ years of animal research behind it, a mechanism that makes sense to me, and a reputation in biohacking circles that is hard to ignore.

**Avoid opening with:**

> BPC-157 is not FDA-approved and has limited human data.

That information belongs in the article, but it should not be the emotional frame of the article.

---

## Positive interpretation is allowed

The writer may say:

- "The animal data is hard to ignore."
- "The mechanism makes sense to me."
- "This is one of the more interesting peptides in this category."
- "I can see why this caught on."
- "This is worth watching."
- "The human work is moving in the right direction."
- "This is not random internet nonsense."
- "The biology is genuinely interesting."

These are interpretations, not medical claims.

## Boundaries

### Do not say:

- "BPC-157 heals tendons."
- "BPC-157 repairs the gut."
- "BPC-157 is proven safe."
- "BPC-157 works."
- "You should try this."
- "This is recommended."
- "This protocol is effective."
- "This vendor is safe because they have a COA."

### Do say:

- "In animal models…"
- "Community-reported…"
- "The proposed mechanism…"
- "My read is…"
- "The research is early…"
- "The human data is still limited…"
- "This is worth watching…"
- "No validated medical dosing standard exists."

---

## Warnings and concerns

Concerns should be stated once, factually, then the article should move on.

Do not circle back to the same warning over and over.

**Bad pattern:**

Human evidence is thin. Later: human evidence is very thin. Later: human evidence is extremely limited. Later: talk to your doctor. Later: remember, human evidence is thin.

**Correct pattern:**

The cumulative human evidence is fewer than 60 participants. That is early. Here is what exists, here is what it means, and here is why the research arc is still worth watching.

---

## Doctor mention rule

Mention talking to a qualified healthcare provider once, usually in the bottom line or disclaimer.

Do not repeat the doctor warning in every section.

**Correct:**

> Are there personal medical reasons this is not a fit for me? That is a doctor conversation. Always is, with anything like this.

Then the disclaimer can include:

> Anyone considering peptides should talk with a qualified healthcare provider.

**Avoid:**

- Talk to your doctor before using this.
- Talk to your doctor if you are considering this.
- Talk to your doctor because this is not medical advice.
- Talk to your doctor again in the bottom line.

That feels anxious and repetitive.

---

## Reader-respect rule

Trust the reader.

The reader is an adult. They can weigh facts, animal research, human research, regulatory status, vendor quality, and uncertainty.

Use language like:

> Adults reading this can take that information and make their own call.

Do not talk down to the reader or overexplain obvious caveats.

## Evidence framing

### Animal research

Animal research should not be dismissed.

**Correct framing:**

- "The animal evidence is unusually strong for an early-stage compound."
- "This is where the BPC-157 case is at its strongest."
- "Animal data does not prove human outcomes, but it is not meaningless either."

**Avoid framing animal research as:**

> "Only animal studies."

That sounds dismissive and loses the reader who is curious about mechanisms.

### Human research

Human research should be framed as "early," not "worthless."

**Correct:**

- "The human research is early but starting to accumulate."
- "This is roughly where I would expect a peptide of this stage to be — animal-rich, human-thin, but moving."
- "The marketing claims are running ahead of the human data, which is normal in this space and worth being clear-eyed about."

**Avoid:**

> "There is no meaningful evidence."

That may be academically defensible in some contexts, but it is not the site's voice unless a peptide truly has no meaningful research foundation.

### Mechanisms

Mechanisms can be exciting.

**Correct:**

- "The mechanism makes sense to me."
- "This is the part of the story I find most compelling."
- "That kind of biology makes me lean in rather than tune out."

Just make sure the mechanism is labeled correctly:

- proposed
- animal-derived
- mechanistic
- not proven in humans

---

## Community use framing

Community sections should document without endorsing.

Use a label like:

> *Community-reported uses — not endorsements*

**Correct phrasing:**

- "The most-discussed community protocol clusters around…"
- "People commonly report using it for…"
- "The 'Wolverine stack' is community shorthand for…"
- "No validated medical dosing standard exists. Every protocol you see online is community-built."

**Avoid:**

- Recommended dose
- Best protocol
- How to use
- Optimal cycle

---

## Regulatory framing

Regulatory sections should be current, clear, and not fear-based.

**Correct:**

- "BPC-157 is in the most active regulatory window of its existence."
- "Removal from Category 2 is not approval."
- "The July 2026 PCAC consultation is the inflection point I would watch most closely."
- "If that consultation opens a clearer pathway, this could become a peptide with a real regulatory lane."

**Avoid:**

> "This is illegal/dangerous/unsafe."

Unless that is factually true for a specific context.

## Section-by-section voice rules

### Intro

**Goal:** make the reader curious.

**Pattern:**

> [Peptide] is one of the more interesting peptides I have come across.
>
> It has [interesting research/mechanism/community reputation]. It is discussed for [main use cases]. So I started digging into the research, and here is what I found: [balanced but interested summary].
>
> This page is not a recommendation and not medical advice. It is my working summary of what the research actually shows, what people are using it for, what regulators are saying as of [date], and where the picture is still being painted.

### What it is

Explain simply. Add one personal-interest sentence if natural.

**Example:**

> That origin story is part of what makes it interesting.

### Mechanism

Explain the mechanism plainly, then own the interpretation.

**Example:**

> That is the part of the story I find most compelling.

### Animal research

Lead with strength.

**Example:**

> This is where the case is at its strongest.

Then include limitations as nuance, not as a takedown.

**Example:**

> One nuance worth knowing…

### Human research

State the numbers clearly. Then interpret.

**Example:**

> That sounds tiny, and it is — but I think the framing matters.

Use the "research arc" frame.

**Example:**

> The relevant question is not how this compares with a fully established drug. The relevant question is where it sits in the research arc.

### Community uses

Label clearly. Document patterns. Do not endorse.

**Example:**

> The most-discussed community protocol clusters around…

### Regulatory

Use date-stamped framing.

**Example:**

> Regulatory status is the part of any peptide page that goes stale fastest, so this section is current as of [date].

Close with what to watch next.

### Purity

This is a practical, high-trust section. Be direct.

**Example:**

> This is the part most peptide content skips, and it is the part that matters most practically.

### What isn't settled yet

Use open-question framing, not anxiety framing.

**Example:**

> A few honest open questions worth knowing about, and my read on each:

### Bottom line

Give a real take.

**Pattern:**

> My honest read: [Peptide] is [interpretation]. The [animal/mechanism/regulatory/community] story is [assessment]. The human research is [stage], but [interpretation].
>
> There is a real difference between "[dismissive category]" and "[interesting but early category]." [Peptide] is in the second category.

### Disclaimer

Short. Standard. One doctor mention.

**Example:**

> This page is informational and not medical advice. Biohacking Unlocked is not a medical resource. [Peptide] is not approved by the FDA for any indication unless otherwise stated, and research-use products are commonly labeled "for research purposes only / not for human consumption." Anyone considering peptides should talk with a qualified healthcare provider.

## "My read" reusable mini-patterns

Use these as the bottom-line framing depending on the peptide category:

### For promising but early peptides

> My honest read: this is not random internet nonsense, but it is also not settled human medicine. The animal research is real, the mechanism is interesting, and the human work is early. That puts it in the "worth watching" category for me.

### For overhyped peptides

> My honest read: this one has more marketing energy than research weight behind it right now. The idea is interesting, but I would want to see much better human data before treating the claims seriously.

### For approved peptide drugs

> My honest read: this is a completely different category from research peptides. Once a peptide has gone through large human trials and FDA approval, we are no longer talking about biohacking theory — we are talking about medicine with a formal label, known benefits, and known risks.

### For community stacks

> My honest read: I can see the logic of the stack on paper, but the combination research is not there yet. The community built the protocol before clinical research caught up.

### For purity/vendor sections

> My honest read: vendor quality is the practical bottleneck in this entire space. The peptide can be interesting on paper, but if the vial is mislabeled, underdosed, contaminated, or backed by a fake COA, the research discussion becomes almost irrelevant.

## Voice for non-standard page types

The standard peptide-page voice template applies to research-chemical peptides where the research is animal-rich and human-thin (BPC-157, TB-500, KPV, etc.). Two other content categories on the site need voice adjustments.

### FDA-approved peptide drugs (Semaglutide, Tesamorelin, Bremelanotide, Elamipretide)

These pages are a different category and should not use the standard "early research" framing. The research is no longer early. The compound has a formal label, known benefits, and known risks.

**Voice adjustments for approved-drug pages:**

- Drop "research is early" framing — use "here's what the trials actually showed"
- Replace "what isn't settled yet" with "what is still being studied" (post-approval surveillance, long-term outcomes, off-label uses)
- The "human research reality" section reverses — these pages have substantial human data, often tens of thousands of participants
- Bottom-line framing uses the **approved peptide drugs** mini-pattern from above
- Still apply: paramedic frame, doctor mention once, trust the reader, plain English
- Still apply: vendor/purity section if the compound has a gray-market parallel (e.g., Bremelanotide has approved Vyleesi vs. gray-market PT-141)

**Example opening for approved drug page:**

> Semaglutide is the peptide that broke containment.
>
> It started as a diabetes drug, then became one of the most-prescribed weight-loss medications in modern history. Unlike most peptides on this site, semaglutide does not need a "the research is early" caveat. It has been studied in tens of thousands of people across multiple Phase 3 trials. The questions about semaglutide are not "does it work?" — they are "what does long-term use look like, who are the right candidates, and what should the GLP-1 conversation actually cover?"

### Cautionary content (closed vendors, fake-COA exposes, regulatory enforcement actions)

The standard "lead with what's interesting, not what's risky" rule applies differently here. For cautionary content, the **interesting part is the cautionary story itself** — the fraud mechanics, the regulatory case, the pattern of behavior, the timeline of how a vendor collapsed.

**Voice adjustments for cautionary pages:**

- Lead with the interesting forensic angle, not generic warnings
- Document evidence factually with citations, dates, court records, primary sources
- Use ChatGPT's safer hedge language for serious accusations: "alleged," "reported," "documented," not "confirmed scam" unless official records exist
- Apply Source E's framing: "We evaluated this and here are the unresolved concerns" rather than "this is a scam"
- The writer's interpretive voice still applies, but the interpretation is about the *case*, not the compound
- Reader-respect still applies — show readers how to recognize the pattern themselves rather than just telling them which vendors to avoid

**Example opening for cautionary page:**

> Peptide Sciences shut down in March 2026, and the most interesting part of the story is what came next.
>
> The company that ran the highest-volume peptide shop in the US for over a decade voluntarily closed. Then their own website started warning customers that anyone selling under the Peptide Sciences name was unauthorized and fraudulent. Successor sites started appearing within weeks. This page documents what happened, who is impersonating the closed brand, and how to verify whether a "Peptide Sciences" link in 2026 is legitimate or a clone.

The cautionary voice is closer to investigative journalism than research interpretation. Same writer, different mode.

## Final publishing checklist

Before publishing any peptide page, confirm:

- [ ] Facts and opinions are clearly separated.
- [ ] Every major study claim names author and year.
- [ ] Animal evidence is labeled as animal evidence.
- [ ] Human evidence includes sample size where possible.
- [ ] Community protocols are labeled community-reported, not advice.
- [ ] Doctor/healthcare-provider mention appears once, not repeatedly.
- [ ] Regulatory status has a date.
- [ ] FDA approval status is clear.
- [ ] WADA status is included if relevant.
- [ ] Category 2 removal is not framed as FDA approval.
- [ ] Purity/vendor section includes COA and batch-specific testing.
- [ ] The page has a real "my honest read" bottom line.
- [ ] The tone is curious, positive, and plain-English.
- [ ] The article does not sound like a medical journal, legal disclaimer, or supplement ad.
- [ ] If the page is an approved-drug page, "early research" framing is removed and the appropriate mini-pattern is used.
- [ ] If the page is cautionary content, the interesting forensic angle leads and "alleged/reported" hedge language is applied to serious accusations.
